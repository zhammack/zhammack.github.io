Priest Town, USA

The Lincoln Diocese ordained more priests than others it's size.

By Zach Hammack

The Catholic Diocese of Lincoln is one of the leading dioceses in the US for ordaining new priests, according to the Center for Applied Research in the Apostolate.

From 2010 to 2013, the diocese ordained 22 priests – the eighth most out of American dioceses.

It also boasts the second lowest Catholic-to-ordinand ratio, with 4,434 Catholics per every newly ordained priest.

For Santiago Izquierdo, a first-year seminarian at St. Gregory the Great Seminary in Seward NE., the numbers aren’t surprising.

“Lincoln is a very traditional diocese,” Izqueirdo said, “and I think that contributes to a fostering of vocations.”

Despite outputting new priests to rival larger dioceses such as New York or Los Angeles, the Diocesan population –including non-Catholics –has stayed fairly steady.

In 1950, for example, the population was at 556,687. By 2013, that number had only increased by around 40,000.

The Catholic population in the Diocese, however, has grown significantly – from around 39,000 Catholics in 1950 to nearly 100,000 in 2013.

Matthew Schilmoeller, who is also a seminarian at St. Gregory the Great Seminary, believes the diocese’s conservative stances helps draw in seminarians.

“The Diocese of Lincoln has upheld the status of an all-male altar,” Schilmoeller said, “and the truth is it has made us extremely blessed with vocations and aspirations of young men to the priesthood.”

An all-male altar –where only boys are allowed to administer the priest during Mass – is rare in the United States. In fact, Lincoln is the only diocese in the US that doesn’t allow girls to serve at the altar, despite it being normalized by the Catholic Church in 1994.

Izqueirdo has no problem with the diocese’s stance because he sees a correlation between the number of ordained priests and an all-male altar.

“Actively participating in the Mass by altar serving leads to that initial thought of becoming a priest,” Izqueirdo said. “And the numbers show that in Lincoln it works.”
